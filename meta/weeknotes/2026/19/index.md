## Our Procedure Browsable Space now Searchable

In terms of changes at the tip of our assorted value chains, perhaps the most immediately noticeable is our [Procedure Browsable Space](https://api.parliament.uk/procedure-browser)&trade; gaining a new ability in the form of search. That's right, you read quite correctly, our Procedure Browsable Space now comes fully-equipped with search functionality. That being put in place by [Shedcode](https://shedcode.co.uk/) [James](https://mastodon.me.uk/@jamesjefferies) across [work packages](https://api.parliament.uk/procedure-browser/work-packages/search?search=health), [enabling legislation](https://api.parliament.uk/procedure-browser/enabling-legislation/search?search=health), [organisations accountable to Parliament[https://api.parliament.uk/procedure-browser/organisations-accountable-to-parliament/current/search?search=health] and [business steps](https://api.parliament.uk/procedure-browser/step-types/Jwc6nqJi/search?search=health). Not only can you now search across more facets than our [statutory instrument](https://statutoryinstruments.parliament.uk/) and [treaty tracking](https://treaties.parliament.uk/) websites, this search is punctuation agnostic, so you shouldn't hit the same "smart" quotes problems you may have stumbled over on our more official offerings.

Offstage, Librarian Jayne continues to ponder whether an integrated search over all searchable facets might be required. Until that's decided, we can only offer our usual gratitude to James for another job well done.

## Toward a single subject view of the Library

Sticking with James and the subject of jobs well done, the lad also finds himself in the number two spot of our 'weekly' hit parade. Why not just hand him the Librarian of the Week trophy and have done with it? we ask ourselves. Let's try to keep him keen, we answer.

If you've been following along from home, you'll know our crack team of librarians have been working hand in glove with Silver and his [Data Language](https://datagraphs.com/) colleagues to prototype a Knowledge Base for the House of Commons Library. The first output of which has been focussed on front of house librarians appropriately [directing enquiry traffik](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=izyZZFD5a5Y) to backstage research colleagues. You'll also be aware that we can't really 'work in the open' on this one because there's a small amount of personal data involved, authentication of some type being required.

We'd always known that the frontend of the work was properly purely prototype and would need a rebuild. The problem being finding someone with time on their hands to do that. In the meantime, the boss boss boss 'brarian - or *The Librarian* as we call him - was keen to share progress with Library colleagues. Librarian Susannah decided the best bet was to split efforts and run the two tasks - demoing what we've built and rebuilding - in parallel. Whilst redevelopment continued, the prototype effort was quite good enough to tout round and get feedback. That was until the [Parliamentary Computational Section](https://www.parliament.uk/mps-lords-and-offices/offices/bicameral/parliamentary-digital-service/) put their foot down with a firm hand and declared basic authentication verboten. At which point, the prototype stopped working. At least for anyone with a Parliament-issued Windows machine.

What to do then? Paying for Data Language to integrate with Parliament's Single-Sign On was one option, but felt like a hell of a waste when we knew that website code was destined for the recycle bin. At which point, up popped Shedcode James with a different idea. And a much better one.

Putting that plan into action, Silver took a backup of any and all personal data and stripped it from the prototype website. The prototype being personal data free, it was no longer necessary to hide its existence from the web. So Data Language colleagues followed up by removing basic authentication.  Which means Librarian Susannah is now able to demonstrate and gather feedback, with assurances that contact details will follow in due course. First problem mostly solved.

Up in Sheffield, the second problem was also getting solved, James sitting down to learn the [Cypher query language](https://neo4j.com/docs/cypher-manual/current/introduction/) and churn out production-ready pixels. A couple of Service Now tickets later, and the rebuilt website was sitting on the Parliament domain, tucked up behind Cloudflare for protection and adapted to work with Parliament's Single Sign-On solution. If you have a parliamentary login as part of the House of Commons Library and are keen on seeing work in progress - and, *warning*, it is very work in progress - why not [sign in and take a peek](https://library-knowledge-base.parliament.uk/).

Thank you for all your efforts Shedcode James. We'd be lost with you.

Still in Sheffield - or the Properly Northern Estate as we call it these days - Developer Jon continues to slave away in the pixel mines. Back down in Westminster, our crack team of librarians continue to test his efforts. [Trello cards](https://trello.com/b/hP5FLFHA/search-application-development) continue to thunder across the net and chalk dust continues to get kicked up from the baseline.

This week, we're happy to report progress on both search result pages and object pages. Kicking off with the former, Jon's been tinkering with our taxonomy service lookup - [weeknotes passim](https://ukparliament.github.io/ontologies/meta/weeknotes/2026/13/#sweating-the-taxonomy-part-i---query-expansion-on-parliamentary-search) - which is now no longer case sensitive. So a very shouty search for [BRITISH AIRWAYS](https://beta-search.parliament.uk/search?query=BRITISH+AIRWAYS&show_detailed=&sort_by=date_desc&results_per_page=20) resolves to the same taxonomic concept as a slightly more polite search for [British Airways](https://beta-search.parliament.uk/search?query=British+Airways&show_detailed=&sort_by=date_desc&results_per_page=20). That [concept being identified as 4493](https://explore.data.parliament.uk/index.html?endpoint=terms/4493), should anyone be interested.

Jon also appears to have fixed a mysterious mix-up whereby a search for monkey nuts returned results for monkey pox. Which is not a mistake any of us wish to make. Your regular correspondent has gazed at [that card](https://trello.com/c/rhp6zYID/761-search-results-dont-match) and is honestly none the wiser. Will no-one think of the scribe?

Over on our object pages, the labels for both [Select Committee reports](https://beta-search.parliament.uk/objects?object=http%3A%2F%2Fdata.parliament.uk%2Fselectcommittees%2F54929) and [Government responses to Select Committee reports](https://beta-search.parliament.uk/objects?object=http%3A%2F%2Fdata.parliament.uk%2Fselectcommittees%2F78615) are both singularised appropriately, and The Claw - more [weeknotes passim](https://ukparliament.github.io/ontologies/meta/weeknotes/2026/07/#one-step-forward-or-one-step-beyond) - which had gone missing from object pages, is now back and neater than ever.

## Psephologising wildly

Psephological efforts are currently concentrated on solving the problem of how we publish the results of the next general election in a more staged fashion than [last time around](https://electionresults.parliament.uk/general-elections/6). At this point, our dear reader is probably thinking, "'ang on. Didn't you do that last year?" Dear reader, you would not be wrong. Way back in [2025, week 49](https://ukparliament.github.io/ontologies/meta/weeknotes/2025/49/), we were pleased to report that staged publishing had been [built and signed off to the specification agreed](https://ukparliament.github.io/ontologies/meta/weeknotes/2025/49/#psephologising-wildly). Unfortunately, further thought revealed that specification to be, if not exactly wrong, lacking the appropriate level of detail.

Our previous efforts broke the election down into four stages:

* post-dissolution and pre-statements of nomination, we just published a candidates coming soon message

* post-statements of nomination and pre-election, we published the candidate list

* as soon as possible post-election, we published the candidate list with winners picked out

* once all results had been verified, we published *all the numbers*

Apart from the final two states all others were mutually exclusive. Which, on the surface, feels fine. But, should events get the better of us and some natural disaster or the death of a candidate take place, one of the 650 elections might get delayed. In which case, we had no means to publish winners or full results for any of the other 649. Which would be less than ideal. Our new approach captures the publication stage on individual elections within a general election, rather than on the general election in general. As it were. Or at least it will do when we manage to patch the code back together. Which is currently scattered all over the floor of [Michael](https://bsky.app/profile/fantasticlife.bsky.social)'s computer. Again.

## Managing Members

A question that's been bothering us for a wee while is how to handle any advance notice of a Member being suspended. And indeed advance notice of when that suspension period is due to end. Advance notice of suspensions is information that has been known to head in our direction, but we've never been quite sure what effect entering that information into the [Members' Names Information System](https://data.parliament.uk/membersdataplatform/#). Would something go horribly wrong and the Member get flagged as suspended before the suspension actually started? It all felt a little risky. Happily, Librarian Phil has now tested what happens in the staging environment, and, even more happily, it [all works perfectly](https://trello.com/c/v9Lm3RZW/407-can-a-suspension-be-applied-and-revoked-in-advance). The Member remains unsuspended until the suspension period kicks in, is flagged as suspended the moment it kicks in and is back to their full self when the suspension expires. Happy with his answer, Phil has added these new facts to the MNIS manual. Because of course he has.

Which is nice.
